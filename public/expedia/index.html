Selected work

Where the thinking shows up

01
Shopify Email

Send-time optimization: one smart default over a complex choice

Merchants had the content. They didn't have the signal. The scheduling flow had two separate actions, schedule and send, that created unnecessary friction and buried the timing decision. I merged them into a single "Schedule send" interaction to introduce a new AI-assisted recommended send time based on industry benchmark and store data.

The design challenge was trust. A recommendation is only useful if a merchant acts on it without second-guessing the source. A simple tooltip made the rationale visible without overwhelming the decision, and the default nudged toward optimal timing while keeping full control available.

Core principle
The right default is a design decision. Surfacing a recommendation without creating anxiety around it, while keeping the merchant in control, required as much care as the UI itself.
Relevant to Expedia
Traveler communications all carry a timing dimension. Itinerary updates, rebooking prompts, check-in reminders. Each one has a moment where it earns trust or loses it. The design problem is the same: how does a system know when to speak?
02
Shopify Marketing

Campaigns: introducing a new primitive into a running system

Merchants had better analytics than ever. But none of it was organized around how they actually planned. I designed Campaigns, a new Admin-level entity for organizing marketing activity, tracking attribution, and evaluating results across channels.

The surface decisions were straightforward. The hard work was the system underneath: Campaigns touched URL redirects, role-based permissions, attribution logic, and surfaces across multiple teams. Introducing a new concept into a mature platform means every downstream consequence becomes a design decision.

Core principle
The model can be complex. The surface cannot. As requirements stacked up, deliberately pushing complexity back into system logic, rather than exposing it, became the primary design job.
Relevant to Expedia
Traveler communications span booking, servicing, and post-trip. A governance layer that organizes what's transactional, promotional, and support-related, making that distinction invisible to the traveler, requires this kind of primitive thinking.
03
Shopify Analytics

Annotations: building the foundation before the AI layer is ready

Analytics could show merchants what changed. Not why. The brief was ambitious and loosely defined: automated events, manual notes, AI-generated callouts, management tooling, with no clear sequencing or scope. My first move was turning that ambiguity into a phased roadmap.

Starting with manual annotations on line charts, I prototyped the MVP in code using Cursor, cloning the Polaris Viz component structure to test interactions in context rather than static Figma. The point was to solve the core act of adding context first, and let that reveal the right questions for automation, AI, and scale.

Core principle
Early ambiguity is a design problem worth solving before writing a single screen. I audited competitive patterns, used an internal AI research tool trained on past merchant interviews to generate realistic scenarios, and built a roadmap that separated the broader vision from the smallest useful starting point.
Relevant to Expedia
Communication systems that will eventually be AI-driven need a well-defined manual foundation first. The sequencing and governance decisions made early shape what intelligent automation can build on. This is that kind of early work.
The AI layer

Systems designed
to learn forward

The annotations work was explicitly about building Phase 1 so that AI-generated callouts and automated event markers could be Phase 2. The same logic applied to Exploration Modes, a project I led design on that introduced new analytical lenses directly into Shopify's reporting interface. Until those modes existed, Sidekick couldn't follow a merchant's question wherever it led. The surface was limiting both the merchant and the AI.

For Expedia's communication systems, the same principle applies: design a governance layer that can eventually be informed by behavioral signals like traveler history, trip phase, and channel preference, without those signals creating noise on the surface. The intelligence lives in the system. The traveler experiences clarity.

In my own process, I use AI to accelerate early exploration, prototype, and synthesize research and documentation. Allowing me to quickly generate structured insights and focus more on design decisions and system thinking.

Manual before automated
AI-assisted communication earns trust when the manual system already works. I build the foundation before the intelligent layer, so automation improves a proven pattern rather than masking a broken one.
Legible recommendations
Send-time optimization showed me that a recommendation is only useful when the merchant understands why. Designing for AI means designing the rationale alongside the output.
Composable primitives
Campaigns wasn't a feature. It was a layer others build on. When AI eventually drives communication logic, it needs a system of well-defined concepts underneath. I design those concepts first.
AI as a design tool
I use AI-assisted research tools, coded prototypes, and agents in my own process, not to shortcut craft, but to test assumptions with less friction and surface edge cases earlier.
